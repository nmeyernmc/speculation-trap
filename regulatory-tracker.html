No Framework

Five years ago, most countries had no rules for crypto at all. Today, the regulatory landscape is a patchwork — some nations have built comprehensive frameworks, others have outright bans, and dozens are still somewhere between a whitepaper and a law. The problem: people are already trading, lending, and losing money in all of them.

United States

GENIUS Act

Signed July 17, 2025. First comprehensive federal stablecoin law. Restricts issuance to banks, credit unions, and licensed non-bank issuers under OCC oversight.

1:1 Reserves BSA/AML State Issuers ≤$10B Rules by Jul 2026 Credit Unions Eligible
VS
🇪🇺

European Union

MiCA

Live across all 27 member states since December 2024. One license, continent-wide access. Covers all crypto-assets, not just stablecoins.

1:1 Reserves Asset Segregation Mandatory Licensing Risk Disclosures Consumer Protection

The World Economic Forum notes these two frameworks are converging — both require 1:1 reserve backing, mandatory audits, and AML compliance. But there's a key difference: MiCA covers the entire crypto ecosystem with explicit consumer protections. The GENIUS Act focuses narrowly on payment stablecoins, leaving retail speculation largely to existing (and often inadequate) securities and commodities frameworks.

The CFTC is now drafting purpose-built rules for retail leveraged crypto trading — a new category of exchange registration designed specifically for crypto derivatives. But those rules won't arrive until 2026 at the earliest, leaving a window where retail participants are exposed.

Chapter III
The State
Laboratory
While Congress debated, states started building.

The GENIUS Act created a federal floor, but states can still regulate issuers under $10 billion. Some were already years ahead. Wyoming minted its own stablecoin. New York has required a BitLicense since 2015. California delayed its digital asset law until 2026. The result is a laboratory of approaches — and a preview of where the friction will be.

$10B
The GENIUS Act threshold: state-regulated stablecoin issuers can operate independently below $10 billion. Above that, federal oversight kicks in.

New York — BitLicense

Most stringent state regime. Stablecoins are virtual currency under 23 NYCRR Part 200. DFS issued USD-backed reserve guidance in 2022.

Chapter V
The Consumer
Protection Gap
Regulating stablecoins is not the same as protecting the people who trade them.

Here's what connects this tracker back to the Speculation Trap: most regulatory frameworks focus on stablecoin issuance and AML compliance — not on the retail investors losing money. Having a well-regulated stablecoin doesn't help the 73–81% of crypto traders who lose money, or the 96% of sports bettors who end up in the red.

The gap between "we regulate the product" and "we protect the person" is where the damage happens.

The Race Isn't Over

By July 2026, U.S. regulators must finalize GENIUS Act rules. The CFTC is drafting frameworks for retail crypto leverage. The ECB is building the digital euro. China's digital yuan is at $2.37 trillion. The question isn't whether regulation is coming — it's whether it arrives before the next generation of retail participants loses their savings in the gap between "allowed" and "protected."
